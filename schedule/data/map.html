Newman Alumni Center

It houses the offices of Public Relations, Alumni Affairs, Continuing Education and the GSW Foundation. In November 2000, the Board of Regents approved the University’s proposal to name the facility in recognition of Jerry and Marjorie Newman of Columbus, Georgia. Jerry Newman is a GSW graduate and Foundation trustee, whose generous donation funded the renovation.

    • Chick-fil-A and Boar’s Head Deli Parking

    Parking in this lot is available only for restaurant patrons without a GSW parking decal (faculty, staff or student). Otherwise, parking decals are required for all visitors, students, faculty and staff. Decals can be obtained from the campus safety office during regular office hours. Visitors may park in "X" or "B" zone, or in designated visitor parking areas. Parking zones are enforced only on school days and only between 8:00 am and 5:00 pm with the exception of no parking zones (yellow curbs), handicapped parking zones, and parking in the area of the Health Center. Parking areas are subject to change. Please obey all posted signs and rules.

    An attractive new residence for the president of Georgia Southwestern was completed in 1966 at a cost of $62,820. It is currently occupied by the Southwest Georgia Court Appointed Special Advocates.

    • Deriso Swimming Pool

    This Olympic-sized covered pool was completed in 1991. It is named in honor of Doris B. Deriso, a major contributor for the facility.

    • Westbrook Bell Tower

    The Daniel Webster Westbrook Memorial Bell Tower is located on the lawn in front of Morgan Hall. It was a gift to the university by lifelong educator and GSW alumna Martha Westbrook in honor of her father, Daniel Webster Westbrook.

    Southwestern Pines

    At 81,000 square feet, this apartment-style residence hall has over 230 beds. It is used for upperclassmen, and was completed in April 2007.

    Highlights of the two-story structure include a convocation hall/sports arena with seating for 2,000; updated athletic facilities, including locker areas, training areas and a fitness center; recreational facilities, including racquetball courts and a climbing wall; new and improved Health and Human Performance facilities, including classrooms, conference areas, and fitness areas.

    • Student Success Center

    The Student Success Center is home to the division of Student Affairs, the Athletic Department, the Office of Continuing Education, and several academic classrooms. During the summer of 2014 the Canes Den was renovated and expanded to make room for a new Chick-fil-A and Boar’s Head Deli combination restaurant.

    • Collum Hall

    Collum Hall was completed in 1952 with the total cost of the building and furnishings listed at $166,800. Collum Hall is now the home of the South Georgia College Entry Program, along with additional GSW classroom space.

  • Student Support Services

    • Crawford Wheatley Hall

    Crawford Wheatley Hall, named for the Honorable Crawford Wheatley who became treasurer of the institution’s Board of Trustees, was constructed during the years of 1911 and 1912 at a cost of $17,500. It is now the home for the growing School of Computing & Mathematics.

  • Department of Computer Science
  • Department of Mathematics

    • Wheatley Administration Building

    Academic Hall was built in 1907 on the Third District A&M campus at a cost of approximately $28,000. The current building was completed in 1918 at a cost of $75,000. It is named in honor of Charles Huntington Wheatley (son of Crawford Wheatley) who died in 1991, bequeathing 55 percent of his $20 million-plus estate to the university.

    • Florrie Chappell Gymnasium

    The main floor served as a combined auditorium and basketball court with a swimming pool facility in the basement. It was named in honor of Florrie Allen Chappell, mother of Allen Chappell. It is not currently in use and is in need of historic renovation.

    Science Building

    Completed in 1965 at a cost of $300,000, this building provided science concentration within a main building. Enrollment and program growth soon rendered this structure inadequate, paving the way for a much larger addition, Roney Building, in 1971.

  • Department of Biology
  • Department of Chemistry
  • Department of Geology & Physics

    • Joseph C. Roney Building

    This 30,000 square-foot addition to the Science Building was completed in 1971 at a cost of $900,000. At the time of its construction, it was described as “one of the best designed and equipped science facilities in the state.” It was named in honor of Joseph C. Roney whose family made possible the J.C. Roney Family Student Assistance Fund.

  • Department of Biology
  • Department of Chemistry
  • Department of Geology & Physics

    • Jackson Hall

    When Georgia Southwestern became a four-year college, new science buildings were constructed, freeing classroom space in Jackson Hall for the music program. On Oct. 12, 2002, the university held a formal ceremony kicking off a $2.7 million renovation of Jackson Hall. That renovation was completed in March 2004.

  • Department of Music
  • Concert Recitals/Special Events

    • Academic Center for Excellence

    In 1962, this building was completed at a cost of $350,000 to serve as a library/classroom building. The library was relocated in 1971 to the James Earl Carter Library facility. Currently, the building houses the Academic Resource Center, Asian Studies, and the GSW TV facilities.

    • Mary Lou Jordan Hall

    Originally intended as a women’s residence hall, this facility was completed in 1967 at a cost of $421,000. It is named in honor of Mary Lou Jordan, who taught English primarily during her 19-year tenure (1926-1945) at Americus Normal College and Georgia Southwestern. Currently Jordan Hall serves office space for contract custodial staff.

    Sanford Hall

    Sanford Hall served as a residence facility until 1970 when it was converted to administrative and service functions. It was completely renovated in 1999 at a cost of $1 million. Currently housed in this facility is the Office of Financial Aid, Human Resources, the Office of Disability Services & Testing and the Registrar's Office.

  • Human Resources
  • Financial Aid
  • Registrars Office
  • Office of Disability Services & Testing

    • Morgan Hall

    Morgan Hall has served as a residence hall for both men and women and eventually as a location for various campus services. An extensive renovation of Morgan Hall was completed in 1995 at a cost of $448,291. The facility currently houses offices including the Office of Information & Instructional Technology.

  • Informational & Instructional Technology

    • Marshall Student Center

    A student union building was completed in 1966 at a cost of $825,000. It was named in honor of the late Thomas O. Marshall, a highly respected citizen, lawyer and banker of Americus. It houses the student dining hall, the campus bookstore, a coffee shop and the offices of Business and Finance and Student accounts. The Marshall family philanthropy has long benefited Georgia Southwestern.

  • Student Accounts
  • GSW Card Services
  • Dining Services

    • Herschel Smith: Public Safety

    Named in honor of Hershel Smith, Georgia Southwestern's first college physician, this $147,000 structure was completed in 1966 and provided complete infirmary services. Dr. Smith served the GSW campus from 1936 to 1961 and was instrumental in the establishment of a nursing program at Georgia Southwestern. The building now serves as the headquarters for GSW's campus safety.

  • Public Safety

    • Beth King Duncan Hall

    Originally planned to accommodate 300 women, this now co-educational facility was completed in 1969 at a cost of $1.1 million. Its name honors Beth King Duncan, who during her 20-year GSW teaching career (1947-1967) taught art, education and history. This space currently serves as an overflow residence hall.

  • Residence Life

    • English Building

    Completed in 1969 at a cost of $820,000, this facility was known as the Academic Building, currently the English Building. At the time of its completion, it was the largest single classroom building for the campus.

  • Dept of English/Modern Language
  • Sirocco

    • Business/History & Political Science

    This building was completed in 1973 at a cost of $1.1 million. It currently houses the School of Business Administration and the Department of History and Political Science. It underwent renovations in 2005 (third floor) and 2008 (second floor).

  • School of Business Administration
  • Dept of History/Political Science

    • Fine Arts Building

    The Fine Arts Building, costing $1.6 million, was completed and opened for classes with the 1982 Winter Quarter. It was designed to house art and drama, and includes a 270-seat theater.

  • Dept of Theater, Communication & Media Arts
  • Department of Visual Arts
  • Dramatic Arts Events
  • Visual Art Events

    • College Lake

    In November 1949, the site for College Lake was cleared. Plans detailed an eight-acre lake and a 13 foot dam with water depth from 10 feet at the dam to four feet in the upper part of the lake. Four springs behind Florrie Chappell Gym were to provide water. Facilities for fishing, boating, swimming and picnicking were included.

  • Recreation Facilities

    • Lake House

    During President William B. King’s tenure (1963-1978), a rustic Lake House was provided, primarily for student social functions. Through the years, this facility has been remodeled and improved.

  • Recreation Facilities

    • Maintenance/Warehouse

    A maintenance shop and warehouse, located on the perimeter road near the lake, was completed in 1967 at a cost of $65,000. Storage and other additions were later authorized with a $133,000 addition in 1971. It has since undergone a $1.4 million renovation/addition.

  • Materials Management
  • Maintenance Request

    • Intramural Field

    GSW Campus Recreation offers a wide variety of athletic events for students, faculty, staff and members on the Intramural Field. Sports such as Flag Football, Volleyball, Ultimate Frisbee and more will be offered throughout the year.

  • Intramural Sports

    • James Earl Carter Library

    This 60,000 square-foot facility was completed in 1971 at a cost of $1.5 million. It is named in honor of James Earl Carter (1894-1953) who was a Sumter County farmer and businessman, civic leader, strong supporter of education, and representative from Sumter County to the General Assembly of Georgia. Carter was the father of the 39th President of the United States, Jimmy Carter.

  • Library
  • Art Gallery

    • Southwestern Oaks I

    This 108,000 square foot, suite-style residence hall has over 400 beds. It was Phase I of a two-phase $30 million residence hall project designed to increase and modernize student housing on GSW’s campus. Located on the South end of campus, Southwestern Oaks is used primarily for freshmen. It was opened in August 2006.

    • Southwestern Oaks II

    This 108,000 square foot, suite-style residence hall has over 400 beds. It was Phase I of a two-phase $30 million residence hall project designed to increase and modernize student housing on GSW’s campus. Located on the South end of campus, Southwestern Oaks is used primarily for freshmen. It was opened in August 2006.

    • Magnolia I

    This 88,000 square foot, suite-style residence hall was constructed for $14 million to accommodate historic enrollment growth at Georgia Southwestern. Built on the site of the vacant Complex III residence hall, Magnolia has 300 beds and houses upperclassmen. Completed in less than seven months, it was opened in August 2009.

  • Residence Halls
  • Southwestern Magnolia

    • Magnolia II

    This 88,000 square foot, suite-style residence hall was constructed for $14 million to accommodate historic enrollment growth at Georgia Southwestern. Built on the site of the vacant Complex III residence hall, Magnolia has 300 beds and houses upperclassmen. Completed in less than seven months, it was opened in August 2009.

  • Southwestern Magnolia

    • Education Center

    The $3.9 million Education Center was completed and occupied Winter Quarter, 1990. In addition to classrooms and faculty offices, the building houses the Early Childhood Development Center and Learning Analysis Center. Complete with a vaulted atrium that echoes the Wheatley Administration Building’s rotunda, it is the site of many evening and continuing education classes.

  • School of Education
  • Early Childhood Development Center

    • Carter I

    In addition to classroom and office space, this 46,000 square foot nursing building boasts a 150-seat auditorium and a state-of-the-art simulation and virtual learning center equipped with lab space for pediatric, trauma, medical/surgical, intensive and primary care, thanks to a $1 million dollar gift from Phoebe Putney Health System. The University Health Center is located within Carter I near the complex’s center courtyard. This building was opened in August 2012.

  • School of Nursing
  • Health Clinic

    • Carter II

    At roughly 35,000 square feet, this building is home to the Rosalynn Carter Institute for Caregiving, the psychology and sociology department, and additional lab space for the sciences. The Psychology/Sociology portion of the building includes five large classrooms, lab space, a clinical counseling room, and a computer testing room. Carter II was opened in January 2013.

  • Dept of Psychology & Sociology
  • Rosalynn Carter Institute

    • Griffin Bell Golf

    A public 18-hole golf course owned and operated by Georgia Southwestern State University. Formerly known as Americus Country Club.
